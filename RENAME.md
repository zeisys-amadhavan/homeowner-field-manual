# The Homeowner's Field Manual — Concept v3

**Working Positioning**
**Title:** The Homeowner's Field Manual
**Subtitle:** The Visual Guide and Working Record for the House You Own — Learn It. Document It. Defend It.
**Format:** 5×8 inches, full color, KDP paperback, ~$8 print cost, target retail $24.99–29.99, target ~140–150 pages.
**Primary buyer:** New homeowners (first 12 months). Secondary: gift-giver buyers (realtors, parents, friends). Tertiary: experienced homeowners who want to start documenting properly.
**Useful life:** 15+ years per copy. The book is intended to live with the house, not just be read once.

## Strategic Foundation — Why This Book Exists in This Form

This section is for the author, not the reader. It captures the positioning decisions so the book stays disciplined while it's being written. Reread before every writing session.

### What this book IS

This is a **hybrid reference + working record**: half educational orientation (how your house works and where it fails) and half standardized forms the homeowner fills in to document their specific house.

The premise: every house is unique, but nobody hands the homeowner a spec sheet. This book is that spec sheet — a standardized, fillable, visual document of truth that captures *what this specific house contains, where everything is, when work was done, and what was paid for it.*

The book becomes the canonical at-a-glance reference for the homeowner, their contractors, their insurance, their next owner, and themselves seven years from now when they cannot remember when the water heater was installed.

### What this book is NOT

**This is not a repair manual.** Black & Decker, Family Handyman, Creative Homeowner, and Reader's Digest own the repair shelf with 400–600 page encyclopedias, 3,000+ photographs, and decades of brand equity. YouTube wins individual repairs in 4-minute videos. We do not compete on either front.

**This is not a "how your house works" encyclopedia.** Charlie Wing's *How Your House Works* already owns that category. He's an MIT PhD who hosted a PBS series and developed Home Depot's 1-2-3 books. Going page-for-page against his depth is a fight we lose.

**This is not a survival or prepper book.** Natural-event content stays in the lane of *house + insurance + documentation*, not go-bags and food storage.

**This is not a binder.** We do not try to hold the full warranty card, full permit document, or full insurance policy. Those live in a folder or cloud drive. The book holds the *standardized summary* of each.

### Why this approach beats the alternatives we considered

Across this concept's iterations, we tried: a 150-page generalist manual (too direct a fight with Wing and Black & Decker), a 125-page first-year orientation (still pure content, easily replaced by a YouTube playlist), and a full binder (production becomes prohibitive on KDP). The hybrid form/reference approach is the version that:

1. **Cannot be replaced by YouTube** — videos do not become the record of YOUR house
2. **Cannot be matched by Wing or repair manuals** — they sell pure content, not a working artifact
3. **Sticks to the buyer once they start filling it in** — switching costs become infinite after page 14 has a warranty number written on it
4. **Justifies its price** — the work product is in the book, not just the wisdom
5. **Survives the 5×8 KDP color format** — no specialty printing required

### The moat — what nobody else has

The form pages themselves are the moat. Nobody publishes a standardized intake form for the homeowner's electrical service, breaker map, plumbing layout, HVAC equipment, roof history, foundation notes, HOA references, insurance summary, or natural-hazard profile. Wing teaches you how electrical works. Repair manuals teach you how to fix outlets. **Nobody publishes "here is the form to document YOUR electrical system."** That is an empty shelf.

The natural-hazard insurance pages, the HOA reference pages, the document inventory pages, and the project paper-trail pages all retain their moat status from previous versions — but now they are also fillable forms, which doubles their utility.

### The contractor protocol — the killer feature

Every form in this book has a **stable ID** (E-1 for electrical service, H-2 for cooling system, R-1 for roof, etc.). The IDs survive editions. They become a shared vocabulary between homeowner and contractor.

When a homeowner contacts an electrician about a problem, the electrician normally has to drag information out of the customer over text. With this book, the homeowner replies: *"Here are photos of Form E-1 and E-2."* The electrician opens the photos and has everything they need — service amperage, panel brand, breaker count, GFCI inventory, age of last inspection — in 30 seconds.

The book includes an early page titled **"For Your Contractor"** that explains the numbering convention in 90 seconds, so any contractor receiving a form photo can immediately understand the system. This makes adoption frictionless: critical mass is not needed. The value exists with N=1 contractor and N=1 homeowner.

Over time, with enough copies in the field, some contractors will start asking for forms by name. That is upside, not the base case. The base case is "my customer just sent me the cleanest service request I've ever received" — which is enough.

### The dual purpose of every system page

Every system in the book serves two purposes simultaneously:

1. **The homeowner becomes knowledgeable.** To fill in the breaker map, they have to actually look at their panel, count slots, identify breaker types, find the grounding. They learn by observation, not by reading. This is genuinely better pedagogy than another reference page.

2. **The book becomes a document of truth.** Once filled in, the form is the answer to every "what kind of system do you have?" question from every contractor, inspector, adjuster, or buyer who ever needs to know.

A working record book is *more* giftable than a reference book, not less. "The book you fill in for your house" is a stronger closing-gift narrative than "a book about your house." The 5×8 size is a feature here — it's pocket/glove-box adjacent and feels personal.

## Core Doctrine

**Know it. Document it. Defend it.**

(We dropped "Maintain it. Fix it." from earlier doctrines. We do not teach maintenance procedures or repair. We teach knowledge, documentation, and defense.)

The five lenses through which every page is written:

- **Shelter** — what protects the people inside
- **Machine** — the moving parts and systems
- **Legal asset** — the property as a legal object
- **Financial asset** — the property as an investment
- **Risk object** — what can happen to it and what protects against it

## The Page Test

Every page must pass at least one of two tests:

1. **The Aha Test** (for educational pages): Would a normal homeowner say, "Darn, I didn't know that"?
2. **The Spec Sheet Test** (for form pages): Five years from now, will the homeowner or their contractor be able to answer a real question by photographing this page?

If a page fails both tests, cut it or rewrite it.

## Book-Level Visual Rules

- **5×8 inches, full color, KDP paperback.** No specialty printing. No lay-flat binding. Perfect-bound is acceptable; people will press it open.
- **One dominant visual per educational page.** Isometric line drawings in the style of the panel diagram (clean technical, no shading drama, components callable out).
- **Educational page on left, form on right where layout allows.** Reader learns and immediately documents.
- **Forms designed for ballpoint pen fillability.** Field sizes must accommodate "200A" or "Carrier 24ACC636A003" without crowding. If you cannot write it in pen at 5×8, the field fails.
- **Every form has its ID printed in the upper-right corner** so a contractor receiving a photo can identify which form they're looking at.
- **Consistent color-coding** across diagrams: water = blue, air = green, electric = red, load = black, heat = orange, pest = yellow.
- **5-Lenses legend corner** on educational pages.
- **No state-specific legal content.** Focus on documents, deadlines, proof, and the *questions to ask*, not the answers.
- **No repair procedures.** Discipline anchor: if a page teaches *how to perform a fix*, it belongs in a different book.

## Form Design Discipline

- **Stable IDs survive editions.** E-1 in 2026 must mean the same form as E-1 in 2036.
- **Every field is necessary, every field is sized.** No "describe your HVAC system" prose blocks — discrete checkable/fillable fields.
- **Paste-in zones are explicitly drawn** with corner marks and dimensions noted (e.g., "2.5" × 3.5" photo area"). Tape or glue stick; not adhesive paper because paper bleed.
- **Date fields on every form** — when first filled in, when last updated.
- **"See also" cross-references** at the bottom of forms where another form is related (e.g., Form E-2 references Form M-3 service log).
- **No locked layouts.** Allow homeowner to draw arrows, scribble margin notes, X out outdated entries when systems change. The book is a working document, not a museum piece.

## Writing Order Suggestion

Don't write in page order. Write in this order to lock in differentiation early and to fail fast on form design before committing to all 50+ forms.

1. **Design 3 hero forms first** — pick the highest-stakes forms and design them completely before writing anything else. Recommended: Form E-1 (electrical service), Form H-1 (heating system), Form ID-1 (property identity). Print them. Try filling them out by hand on a real 5×8 page. Fix the field sizing. *This is the riskiest part of the project — get it right before scaling.*
2. **Write the "For Your Contractor" page (008) and the Form Numbering Convention page (007)**. Lock the system before you scale forms.
3. **Section B (Property Records) and Section H (Next Owner)** — these are the strongest moat sections and define the "this is your house's record" identity of the book.
4. **Section C systems documentation** — do all the forms first, then write the educational openers. This forces the educational content to support the forms, not the other way around.
5. **Sections D and E (logs and projects)** — repetitive layouts, write fast.
6. **Sections A (mindset) and F (emergency)** — these tie everything together; write near the end when the book's voice is locked in.
7. **Section G (year-end reviews) and back matter** — last, mostly templates and indexing.

If at any point the educational pages start drifting into repair territory or into Wing's depth, cut them back. The forms are the moat. The educational pages are the *reason* to fill in the forms — not the product itself.

## Risks and Honest Caveats

For the author to hold in mind:

- **Buyers of "fill-in" books often don't fill them in.** This is normal. Planner and journal categories have the same dynamic. The fantasy of being organized sells better than the reality, and that's fine — but don't over-rely on "users will love it because it's so useful." Some will love it. Most will buy it, fill in 10 pages, then shelf it. Both outcomes pay royalties.
- **The book is a single point of failure for the data.** Mitigate by recommending owners photograph filled pages periodically; explicitly cover this in the front matter.
- **System replacements outpace book design.** When the HVAC is replaced, the old H-1 form is now wrong. The convention: draw an X through it, write "see Form H-1 v2 on page [X]" in the margin, and fill in a fresh blank form from the digital companion (taped or three-hole-punched into the back). The book becomes a *log of system generations*, which is more useful than a binder where history is replaced and lost.
- **Digital alternatives exist** (Notion, Evernote, Google Drive). Counter-position by emphasizing artifact value: no subscription, no platform risk, transferable, gift-able, survives data breaches and forgotten passwords. Don't compete on features.
- **Contractor adoption is upside, not the base case.** The book is valuable at N=1 contractor + N=1 homeowner. Critical mass would be wonderful but is not assumed in the business plan.
